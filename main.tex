\section{Grid Computing Architectures}
\subsection{The Architgitectural Design Challenge}
Designing an architecture for Distributed Grid Computing requires resolving a fundamental tension: the architecture must
 be flexible enough to accommodate the enormous heterogeneity of grid resources and use cases, yet standardised enough to
  enable interoperability across administrative boundaries. An architecture that is too rigid will fail to accommodate the 
  diversity of real-world grid deployments; one that is too flexible will produce incompatible implementations that cannot 
  interoperate. The evolution of grid architectures over three decades reflects successive attempts to strike this balance,
   from the pragmatic five-layer model of the original Globus Toolkit, through the service-oriented Open Grid Services 
   Architecture, to the contemporary federated architectures of the European Open Science Cloud \parencite{donvito2023}.

\subsection{The Five-Layer Hourglass Architecture}

\subsubsection{Fabric Layer}
The Fabric layer comprises the physical and logical resources that grid computing aggregates: processors and computational 
clusters, mass storage systems, databases and scientific data archives, network infrastructure, and specialised instruments
 such as radio telescopes and genomic sequencers. Each Fabric layer resource is managed by a local resource management
  system (LRMS) — also called a batch scheduler — that enforces site-specific access policies and executes submitted jobs. 
  Prevalent LRMS platforms include SLURM, HTCondor, PBS/TORQUE, and LSF. Critically, Fabric layer resources retain full
   autonomy under their local administrators; grid architecture does not require sites to surrender control of their 
   resources.
